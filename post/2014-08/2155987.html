It requires only one engine restart to do it that way. The plane change manoeuvre requires less delta-v since it's proportional to the speed at apogee. The satellite perigee is first lifted to 36000 km and the plane changed, then the apogee is lowered from 90000 to 36000 km.
If the satellite were lifted to near escape speed the plane change and perigee lifting would require practically no delta-v, but the orbit would be hyper-sensitive to perturbations by sun and moon and might fall back into the atmosphere. Also, the Falcon 9 probably couldn't boost it to that speed.

To reduce the amount of propellant required to be carried and used by the satellite itself.

To use excess launch vehicle performance to reduce spacecraft propellant

No, the spacecraft would carry the same amount of propellant, it just would have more for stationing keeping and hence missions life instead of using it for plane change.  The spacecraft mass is "fixed' and the excess launch vehicle performance is used to reduce the delta v to be performed by the spacecraft.  If the spacecraft had more capacity for propellant then the excess launch vehicle performance would be used to lift more propellant and not perform SSTO.

Look up bi-elliptic transfer.  Note that a bi-elliptic transfer can reduce the total delta-V needed even if there is no plane change.  It does, however, require three engine burns rather than the two needed for a pure Hohmann transfer.



What's the advantage of a supersynchronous orbit?
I noticed that Thaicom 6 was being delivered to a supersynchronous GTO. What's the advantage of a supersynchronous orbit? It seems to me that if it doesn't need to be in a geostationary orbit, it would save fuel to just deliver it to a "subsynchronous" orbit. Wikipedia says a lot of derelict satellites are put into supersynchronous orbit to prevent re-entry and keep them out of the way of the functioning satellites. But I couldn't figure out why you'd send a functioning satellite out there. I guess its orbit would decay much slower. Any answers?

Apparently SES-8 was delivered to a supersynchronous orbit as well and I found a reason for that here

SES-8 is being inserted into a Super-Synchronous orbit to reduce the total velocity change required by the satellite to reach Geostationary Orbit. A conventional insertion into GEO from an elliptical transfer orbit would require a delta-v of 1,800m/s while the SSTO design reduces the total dV requirement to 1,500m/s.

edit: Just wanted to clarify that SSTO stands for Super-Synchronous Transfer Orbit in this case, not Single Stage to Orbit like it often does.


Changing your inclination at the apogee of a supersynchronous orbit is much more efficient than doing it at GEO altitude, because your velocity at apogee in the SSTO is much lower.

Ariane V (and any other launcher that launches from somewhere near the equator) doesn't have to worry about this because its initial orbit is already nearly equatorial, so it's cheaper to put the satellite in an orbit with apogee at GEO altitude.
